### Chapter 1 — Three Knocks
Rain battered the apartment windows hard enough to sound like fingernails scraping glass. Fourteen-year-old Adrian Lawrence sat alone in the dim kitchen, staring at the digital clock above the microwave.

2:13 AM.

The apartment smelled faintly of burned coffee and cigarette smoke—remnants of his father’s habits lingering like ghosts in the stale air. The television played softly in the living room, though Adrian wasn’t watching it. The late-night news anchor spoke in a dull monotone about a highway accident somewhere north of the city, but the words faded beneath the growing storm outside.

His attention remained fixed on the phone sitting on the kitchen table. Waiting. His father had promised he would call. Again. Daniel Lawrence was always chasing stories no sane person wanted near them : missing hikers, disappearances, cold cases, corrupt officials. But this investigation felt different. For weeks Daniel had barely slept. Adrian had overheard phone conversations late at night—frightened witnesses, trembling motel clerks, truck drivers refusing to speak after sunset. All connected to the same place : GRIDHouse Hotel. A forgotten roadside hotel hidden deep within the forests outside Black Hollow County. People checked in; some never checked out. Others returned changed. Daniel called them “survivors,” but Adrian had seen the photographs spread across his father’s desk. Nobody in those photographs looked alive anymore.
Lightning flashed outside. The phone rang. Adrian nearly jumped from his chair. He grabbed the receiver immediately.
“Dad?”

Static crackled across the line. Then Daniel’s voice emerged through the interference. “Took you long enough,” he joked weakly.

Relief flooded Adrian’s chest. “Where are you?”

A car door slammed somewhere on the other end. “Outside the hotel.”

For a moment neither of them spoke. Rain hammered against Daniel’s windshield. Adrian could hear windshield wipers dragging rhythmically across glass. Back and forth. Back and forth. Faint jazz music played somewhere in the background.

“The place looks abandoned,” Daniel muttered. “Honestly surprised it still has electricity.”

Adrian swallowed nervously. “You really have to go inside?”

Daniel laughed softly, but the sound lacked confidence. “That’s usually how investigations work.”

Another burst of static interrupted him. This time it sounded almost like whispering buried beneath the interference.

Adrian frowned. “Dad?”

“I’m here.” More static. Then something else. A distant metallic sound. Dragging. Like chains scraping concrete. Daniel went silent.

“Dad?” No response. Only breathing. Slow. Uneasy.

Adrian tightened his grip on the receiver. Then he heard it. Three knocks.

THUD. THUD. THUD.

Not from the hotel door. Not from Daniel’s car. From somewhere impossibly close to the phone itself.

Adrian’s blood turned cold. “What was that?”

The line crackled violently. For several seconds only static answered him. Then Daniel whispered: “Something is wrong with this place.” The words barely sounded human.

Another noise echoed through the receiver. Screaming. Distant, distorted, multiple voices overlapping together in impossible agony.

Daniel inhaled sharply.

A deafening metallic bang erupted through the phone. The jazz music warped violently. The screams grew louder.

Adrian stood from his chair. “Dad?\!”
Heavy breathing. Running footsteps. Then—three knocks again. Closer this time.
THUD. THUD. THUD.
Daniel suddenly screamed. Not from pain. From terror.

The line cut dead. Silence flooded the apartment. Adrian stared at the receiver pressed against his ear. The storm outside intensified. Seconds passed. Then minutes. Still he listened. And somewhere beneath the dead static of the disconnected call, something continued knocking. Slowly. Patiently. Waiting.

### Chapter 2 — The Boy Who Never Slept
Seventeen years later, Adrian still slept with the lights on. Not because he feared the dark. Because darkness reminded him of hallways. Long hallways. Endless hallways.
The digital clock beside his bed read 3:01 AM in cold red numbers, casting faint light across the cramped apartment. Adrian lay awake on top of the sheets, staring at the ceiling while rain tapped softly against the windows. Another sleepless night. Another cycle. His eyes burned from exhaustion, but sleep no longer brought rest. Sleep brought corridors. Sleep brought knocking. And sometimes, sleep brought the hotel.
The apartment around him looked less like a home and more like evidence collected by an obsessed detective losing his mind. Newspaper clippings covered the walls : missing persons reports, old photographs, maps marked with red circles and frantic handwriting. At the center of everything was the same image repeated over and over again: GRIDHouse Hotel. The building haunted every corner of his life, even after seventeen years.

Adrian slowly sat up from the bed, rubbing trembling hands across his face. The insomnia had worsened recently. Some nights he heard whispers inside the walls. Other nights he woke up convinced somebody was standing beside his bed watching him breathe. Doctors blamed trauma. Therapists blamed unresolved grief. Medication only made the dreams more vivid. Nothing helped. Because deep down, Adrian knew something terrifying: his father had not simply disappeared. Something had taken him.

The sound of distant thunder rolled across the city. Adrian walked through the apartment toward the kitchen, stepping carefully around stacks of notebooks scattered across the floor. Every notebook contained the same thing: sketches. Doors. Hallways. Room numbers. Always the same number.

He opened the refrigerator. Inside: beer, expired medicine, half a sandwich wrapped in paper towels. Nothing else. He shut the door.

The silence inside the apartment felt wrong tonight. Heavy. As though the room itself were waiting for something. Adrian leaned against the kitchen counter and lit a cigarette with shaky fingers. The flame briefly illuminated the dark circles beneath his eyes. Thirty-one years old, yet he looked much older. Sleep deprivation had hollowed him out from the inside.

He inhaled deeply, then froze. Somewhere inside the apartment, a sound echoed. Soft. Almost inaudible.

Knocking.

Adrian’s heartbeat slowed. No. Not again . He turned toward the hallway leading to the front door. Silence. His breathing became shallow. The cigarette trembled between his fingers.

Then—THUD. THUD. THUD . Three slow knocks.

Adrian dropped the cigarette. His chest tightened instantly. The same pattern. Exactly the same. Just like the phone call seventeen years earlier. For several seconds he couldn’t move. The apartment suddenly felt colder.

He approached the front door carefully. Another thunderclap rattled the windows. The hallway outside remained silent and empty. Adrian unlocked the door and slowly pulled it open. Nobody stood there. Only darkness stretching through the apartment corridor.

His pulse pounded violently in his ears. Then he noticed something near the doorway floor. A small object wrapped in wet brown paper. Adrian stared at it without touching it. Rainwater dripped from the package onto the carpet. Fresh. Meaning someone had left it seconds ago. He quickly looked down both ends of the hallway. Still empty. No footsteps. No elevator sounds. Nothing.

Adrian carried the package inside and locked the door immediately. The room suddenly felt too quiet. He placed the object carefully on the kitchen table. For several moments he simply stared at it, then slowly unwrapped the paper.

Inside lay three objects. A burned photograph. A rusted brass key. And an old cassette tape labeled in faded handwriting: *DANIEL LAWRENCE*.

Adrian’s breathing stopped. His hands immediately began shaking. “No…”

The photograph showed the GRIDHouse Hotel standing beneath heavy fog. But something about the image felt wrong. The building looked… alive. Its windows glowed faintly with warm golden light despite the photograph clearly being decades old. Adrian turned the picture over slowly. Written on the back in black ink were five words: *IT KNOWS YOUR NAME*.

A sudden wave of nausea hit him. His eyes moved toward the rusted key beside the photograph. The tag attached to it was badly corroded, but the number remained visible.

Adrian stared at the key for a long time, then finally reached for the cassette tape. The plastic casing felt ice cold in his hands. His father’s handwriting was unmistakable. A memory suddenly resurfaced in Adrian’s mind—Daniel sitting beside him when he was a child, laughing, recording jokes onto an old tape recorder. The memory vanished as quickly as it appeared.

Adrian swallowed hard. Across the apartment, the television suddenly flickered on by itself. Static filled the room. Adrian slowly turned toward it. The screen flashed violently between static and distorted images: hallways, doors, a dimly lit hotel lobby. Then, for a single frame, a woman standing behind a reception desk smiling directly at him.


The television immediately shut off. Darkness reclaimed the apartment. Adrian stood frozen, his heartbeat thundering inside his chest. Then his eyes slowly drifted back toward the cassette tape waiting on the table.

Outside, thunder roared across the city once more. And somewhere deep within the apartment walls, something knocked back.

### Chapter 3 — The Tape

Rain continued falling outside the apartment like a world slowly drowning. Adrian sat motionless at the kitchen table, staring at the cassette tape beneath the weak yellow light overhead. The room felt smaller now. Too small. The shadows near the corners looked deeper than they should have—alive somehow.

The rusted Room 404 key remained beside the burned photograph, untouched. Adrian could not stop looking at it. The number carved into the metal tag seemed darker each time his eyes returned to it. His father’s room. Or perhaps, his grave.

Adrian clenched his jaw and reached toward an old cardboard box sitting beneath the table. Inside were fragments of a life he had spent years trying to bury: old newspapers, photographs, medical prescriptions, therapy notes, and an ancient cassette recorder covered in dust. He slowly pulled the recorder out. For several seconds he simply held it in silence, his fingers tracing scratches along the plastic surface. He remembered this recorder; Daniel used it constantly while investigating stories. As a child, Adrian used to imitate radio announcers into the microphone while his father laughed in the background. The memory twisted painfully inside his chest.

He inserted the tape. The machine clicked softly. For a moment nothing happened. Only static.

Then, a voice emerged. Tired. Weak. Older than Adrian remembered.

“Testing…” Static crackled violently. Then: “If you’re hearing this, it means I was right.”

Adrian froze. His father. Daniel’s breathing sounded uneven, strained as though he had been running for hours.

“The hotel is not abandoned,” Daniel continued quietly. “It only wants people to think it is.” A loud metallic noise echoed somewhere in the background of the recording. Daniel paused. Adrian leaned closer unconsciously.

“I don’t know how much time has passed since I entered this place,” Daniel whispered. “The clocks stopped working on the second night.” Static hissed across the tape. Then came another sound beneath it—whispering, dozens of overlapping voices speaking too quietly to understand.

Adrian felt goosebumps crawl across his skin. Daniel continued speaking rapidly now. “There are rooms here that shouldn’t exist. Corridors changing direction. Doors leading back to themselves.” Another pause. Then Daniel said something that made Adrian’s blood run cold : “I met a man tonight who claimed he checked into the hotel in 1987\. But he hasn’t aged a single day.”

The apartment lights flickered once. Adrian glanced upward nervously. The tape continued.

“There’s something beneath this place,” Daniel whispered. “Something alive.” A distant banging noise echoed through the recording. Not loud, but deliberate.

THUD. THUD. THUD.

Adrian stopped breathing. The same knocking. Daniel’s voice dropped lower. “I think the staff know what’s happening here. Especially the receptionist.” A sudden burst of static distorted the tape violently. Then Daniel spoke again, only now his voice sounded terrified : “She keeps smiling at empty corners.”

Something crashed loudly in the background of the recording. Daniel inhaled sharply. “Oh God…” Metal scraped across concrete somewhere behind him. The tape picked up faint footsteps—slow, dragging, approaching. Daniel’s breathing became rapid. “If anybody finds this recording,” he whispered desperately, “do not come here. Do you hear me? Don’t ever come here.”

Another bang erupted nearby, closer this time. Adrian realized his own hands were trembling.

Then, a new voice appeared faintly on the tape. Not Daniel’s. A woman. Soft. Calm. Smiling through her words : “Room 404 is waiting.”

Adrian’s eyes widened. The receptionist. He had never heard her before, yet somehow, he recognized the voice instantly.

Daniel cursed under his breath. The tape recorder shifted violently as if he had grabbed it while moving. Running footsteps echoed. Then screaming—far away at first, then suddenly much closer. Not one voice; hundreds. Agonized. Broken. The sound became unbearable. Daniel shouted over the chaos : “IT’S INSIDE THE WALLS\!”

The tape distorted violently. Static swallowed everything for several seconds. Then came the final recording—Daniel whispering, crying : “It knows my name.”

A deep metallic knock echoed nearby. THUD . Daniel gasped. THUD . Closer. THUD . Right beside him. Then Daniel screamed, not from pain, but from absolute terror.

The recording abruptly cut into violent static. Adrian immediately shut the tape recorder off. Silence slammed into the apartment. His chest rose and fell rapidly. The room suddenly felt freezing cold. He stared at the recorder in horror. Something about the tape was wrong—not merely frightening, but impossible. Daniel had disappeared seventeen years ago, but the voice on the tape sounded far older than it should have, like a man trapped somewhere for decades.

Adrian slowly looked toward the burned hotel photograph lying on the table and froze. The image had changed. The hotel no longer appeared abandoned. Warm light glowed from the windows. The front entrance stood open. And on the fourth floor, inside one illuminated window, someone stood watching him. Tall. Motionless. Impossible to fully see through the grainy photograph.

Adrian leaned closer. The figure slowly tilted its head. The apartment lights instantly went out. Darkness consumed the room.

Adrian stood up so quickly the chair crashed backward onto the floor. His breathing became frantic. “Hello?” No answer. Only darkness.

Then, from somewhere inside the apartment, a woman’s voice whispered softly : “Welcome back, Adrian.”

The tape recorder suddenly clicked back on by itself. Static screamed through the darkness. And beneath the noise, someone began knocking from the other side of the walls.

### Chapter 4 — The Endless Highway

The storm followed Adrian out of the city. Rain lashed violently across the windshield as his car cut through the empty highway, headlights barely piercing the ocean of fog gathering ahead. The cassette tape sat on the passenger seat beside him—silent now, but Adrian could still hear his father’s screaming buried somewhere inside his mind. Every few minutes his eyes drifted toward the rusted Room 404 key lying beside the tape recorder. The metal looked darker tonight, wet somehow, as though freshly pulled from deep underwater.

Adrian tightened his grip on the steering wheel. He should have gone to the police, or burned everything, or checked himself into a hospital before driving halfway across the state chasing the ghost of a dead man. Instead, he kept driving deeper into the storm, toward GRIDHouse Hotel, toward the place that had destroyed his father.

The highway signs became increasingly unfamiliar the farther he traveled north. Most roads eventually disappeared into forest-covered hills where civilization slowly dissolved into endless wilderness. The radio hissed softly through static. A jazz song played faintly between bursts of interference. Adrian frowned, recognizing the melody immediately: the same music from the phone call seventeen years earlier. He quickly changed stations. Static. Another station; the same song. Another; still the same. A woman softly singing over old piano keys—calm, slow, almost hypnotic.

Adrian switched the radio off completely. Silence filled the car. For several minutes only the sound of rain remained.

Then Adrian noticed something strange. A gas station appeared ahead through the fog—small, dimly lit, completely empty. He drove past it without slowing. Ten minutes later, the same gas station appeared again.

Adrian’s stomach tightened. Same broken neon sign. Same flickering lights. Same rusted ice machine outside the entrance. Impossible. He checked the rearview mirror; the road behind him vanished into thick white fog. No intersections. No turns. No alternate routes. Just endless highway.

Adrian pressed harder on the accelerator. The speedometer climbed. Rain blurred violently across the windshield, but still the road stretched endlessly ahead. Then another sign emerged through the storm:

WELCOME TO BLACK HOLLOW County. Population: 312\.

The sign beneath it had been scratched repeatedly by something sharp. Adrian drove past it. Twenty minutes later, the same sign appeared again.

His pulse quickened. “No…”

This time he stopped the car. Rain hammered against the roof while the engine idled softly. Adrian stared through the windshield at the county sign ahead. The scratches covering its surface looked almost deliberate—not random damage, but words. He slowly stepped out into the storm. Cold rain soaked through his jacket instantly. The surrounding forest stood unnaturally silent despite the weather: no insects, no birds, nothing. Only fog drifting between the trees like pale smoke.

Adrian approached the sign cautiously. The metal surface was covered in hundreds of deep scratches. Most were unreadable, but several words stood out clearly : TURN BACK. HE IS AWAKE. IT KNOWS YOU.

Adrian stepped backward slowly, then froze. Someone stood across the road—a figure partially hidden within the fog near the tree line. Tall. Motionless. Watching him.

Adrian’s heartbeat stumbled violently. “Hello?” No response. The figure did not move. Rain poured endlessly between them.

Adrian narrowed his eyes. The silhouette appeared human, but something felt deeply wrong about it. Its arms hung too low. Its posture bent unnaturally. And its face… its face looked blurred, as though reality itself refused to focus on it.

Adrian quickly returned to the car and locked the doors. The figure remained motionless near the forest, watching. The headlights illuminated it only partially through the rain. Then suddenly—it was closer. Not walking, not running; simply closer.

Adrian inhaled sharply. The figure now stood near the opposite side of the highway. Its head tilted slowly sideways—too far, too unnatural. Adrian slammed the car into drive and accelerated violently down the road. The tires screeched against wet asphalt. Fog swallowed the figure almost immediately, but Adrian’s hands continued shaking on the steering wheel. His breathing became shallow.

The road ahead twisted sharply through endless forest, trees stretching high above the highway like black skeletal fingers clawing toward the storm clouds. Then the radio suddenly turned on by itself. Static exploded through the speakers. Adrian cursed and reached for the dial—but froze.

A voice emerged through the interference—his father’s voice, weak, distorted : “Adrian…”

Adrian’s entire body went cold. “Dad?”

Static crackled violently. Then: “Don’t stop driving.” The voice sounded distant, like it came from underwater.

“Dad, where are you?\!” For several seconds only static answered him. Then another voice whispered softly through the speakers—a woman, calm, smiling : “He’s been here a very long time.”

The radio abruptly died; silence returned instantly. Adrian’s pulse thundered in his ears. Then the fog ahead slowly parted, and for the first time, he saw it: GRIDHouse Hotel. Standing alone beside the highway—massive, dark, ancient. Its flickering neon sign glowed faintly through the storm: GRIDHOUSE HOTEL. Several windows emitted warm golden light despite the building clearly being abandoned for decades.

Adrian slowed the car unconsciously. Something deep inside him screamed to turn around, to leave, to run. But another feeling rose stronger than fear—recognition, as though some forgotten part of him had been searching for this place his entire life.

Lightning illuminated the hotel for one brief second, and in one of the upper-floor windows, Adrian saw dozens of human silhouettes standing perfectly still behind the glass, watching him arrive. The lightning vanished. The figures disappeared. Only the hotel remained, waiting.

The closer Adrian drove toward it, the quieter the storm became, until finally, the rain stopped completely. Silence swallowed the world. And somewhere deep inside the hotel, three slow knocks echoed through the darkness.

### Chapter 5 — The Receptionist

The hotel parking lot was empty—not abandoned empty, but waiting empty. Adrian slowly stepped out of the car, his shoes crunching against gravel soaked black by rainwater. The cold night air carried a strange smell from the hotel: old wood, dust, and something metallic beneath it, like dried blood hidden beneath perfume. The towering shape of GRIDHouse Hotel loomed above him through drifting fog, its neon sign flickering weakly against the darkness: GRIDHOUSE HOTEL. Several letters buzzed intermittently, casting unstable red light across the cracked pavement.

Adrian stared upward at the building. The architecture looked wrong somehow. At first glance it resembled a normal roadside hotel from the seventies—faded walls, narrow windows, rusted balconies. But the longer he looked, the less sense the structure made. Some windows appeared larger than the rooms behind them should allow. Several floors seemed taller than the others. And near the top of the building, one entire row of windows looked impossibly dark—not unlit, but absent, as though that section of the hotel did not fully exist.

A cold shiver crawled down Adrian’s spine. The wind outside had completely stopped; no insects, no distant traffic, no sound except the faint electrical hum of the hotel sign. Then the front entrance doors slowly opened by themselves.

Adrian froze. Warm yellow light spilled outward from the lobby. Soft jazz music drifted into the night air, and a woman’s voice sang faintly somewhere inside—calm, elegant, wrong.

Adrian swallowed hard. Every instinct told him to leave, but the image of his father screaming on the cassette tape still echoed inside his skull. So he stepped forward. The glass entrance doors closed silently behind him the moment he entered.

The warmth inside the lobby hit him immediately—not comforting warmth, but artificial warmth, like entering a room that had been sealed shut for years. The lobby looked untouched by time. Dark wooden walls reflected golden chandelier light overhead. Old paintings hung along the corridor entrances. Leather chairs surrounded small tables where cups of coffee still steamed softly. Yet something about the scene felt staged, frozen, like a memory endlessly repeating itself. A grandfather clock ticked loudly near the staircase, but the rhythm was uneven: Tick. Tick. Tick Tick. Pause. Tick.

Adrian’s eyes slowly moved across the room. Several people sat throughout the lobby: a businessman reading a newspaper, an elderly couple drinking tea, a woman smoking beside the window. None of them moved—not even slightly. At first Adrian thought they were statues, then the businessman slowly turned a page—too slowly, like someone imitating human behavior without understanding it.

Adrian felt his pulse quicken. The jazz music continued drifting softly through hidden speakers overhead. Then he saw her. Behind the reception desk stood a pale woman dressed in a dark crimson uniform, perfectly still, perfectly poised, watching him.

The Receptionist.

Her skin looked unnaturally smooth beneath the chandelier light—not beautiful, but artificial, like porcelain stretched over something unfinished underneath. Her black hair rested neatly over one shoulder. Her lips curved into a calm professional smile that never fully reached her eyes, because her eyes held no warmth whatsoever—only recognition.

She folded her hands neatly across the desk. “Good evening, Adrian.”

His blood froze instantly. He stopped walking. The receptionist tilted her head slightly; the movement looked too precise, mechanical.

“How do you know my name?”

Her smile widened almost invisibly. “We’ve been expecting you.” The words struck him harder than they should have—not *we are expecting you*, but *we have been*, as though his arrival had been inevitable for a very long time.

Adrian glanced around the lobby again. The guests still barely moved. The elderly woman near the window now stared directly at him without blinking, while smoke continued rising from her cigarette in a perfectly straight line.

“How long has this hotel been open?” Adrian asked quietly.

The receptionist’s fingers tapped softly against the wooden desk. “Time works differently here.”

Adrian felt ice crawl through his stomach. The lights above flickered once. For a split second, the lobby changed. The walls became stained black. The chandelier overhead swung violently. Every guest appeared horribly distorted: skin stretched, mouths hanging open, eyes completely hollow. Then the normal lobby returned instantly.

Adrian staggered backward slightly. The receptionist remained smiling. “Long drive?”

He stared at her. “What is this place?”

Her expression never changed. “A place for people who cannot let go.”

The grandfather clock behind him suddenly stopped ticking. Silence filled the lobby. Adrian slowly realized none of the guests were breathing—not one chest rising, not one blink, nothing. The businessman continued pretending to read his newspaper without moving his eyes.

The receptionist reached beneath the desk calmly, then slowly slid something across the polished wooden counter toward him: a rusted brass key to Room 404\. The moment Adrian saw the number, his chest tightened painfully.

“You already know your room,” she said softly.

“I’m not staying here.”

The receptionist’s smile deepened. “Oh, Adrian…” Her voice lowered almost sympathetically. “You already have.”

The lights flickered violently overhead. For a brief moment the entire hotel groaned around him like an enormous living thing shifting in its sleep. Adrian stepped backward instinctively. The receptionist leaned slightly closer, and for the first time, he noticed something horrifying: her reflection in the mirror behind the desk was not copying her movements. The reflection stood completely still, smiling wider than humanly possible.

Adrian’s breath caught in his throat. The receptionist whispered softly: “Once the door opens…” The chandelier lights dimmed suddenly. “…it remembers you.”

Every guest in the lobby slowly turned toward Adrian simultaneously. The lights went out. Complete darkness swallowed the hotel, and somewhere deep within the endless corridors above, three slow knocks echoed through the building.

### Chapter 6 — Corridor of Eyes

Darkness swallowed the lobby whole. For several seconds Adrian could hear nothing except his own breathing. Then, the jazz music returned softly somewhere far away inside the hotel—a piano playing an old melody through layers of static. The chandeliers flickered back to life one by one with weak golden flashes, but the lobby had changed. The guests were gone; every chair now stood empty. The coffee cups had vanished. The grandfather clock near the staircase no longer existed—only dust remained where it once stood.

Adrian turned slowly in place. The receptionist was gone too. The front entrance behind him had disappeared, and in its place stood only a long narrow hallway stretching deeper into the hotel. The walls looked older now. Rotting wallpaper peeled downward in wet strips. Water stains spread across the ceiling like diseased veins. The warm comforting atmosphere from moments earlier had vanished completely. This was the real hotel, or something closer to it.

Adrian’s pulse thundered in his ears. “Hello?” His voice disappeared into the corridor without echo. No response came back—only silence. Then, a door somewhere far away creaked open slowly. Adrian nearly jumped as the sound echoed unnaturally long through the hallway. He looked down at the rusted Room 404 key still clenched tightly in his hand—cold, ice cold now. He should leave immediately, but the hotel no longer seemed interested in giving him that choice.

The corridor ahead stretched impossibly far, too far for the building’s exterior dimensions. Adrian forced himself forward cautiously, each footstep sounding muffled against the faded carpet beneath him. The hallway lights buzzed softly overhead; some flickered rapidly, while others remained completely dark. Room numbers passed beside him slowly, and then suddenly, Adrian stopped walking.

His stomach tightened. He turned backward. The previous room behind him now read: ROOM 404.

“What…” He looked ahead again. The corridor remained unchanged—long, silent, endless.

Adrian continued walking slowly. The deeper he moved into the hallway, the quieter the hotel became, until eventually, even his own footsteps stopped making sound. The silence became suffocating, like pressure building inside his skull. Then he noticed the wallpaper moving. At first he thought it was a trick of the light, but no—the patterns embedded within the faded wallpaper shifted slowly beneath the surface. Shapes, faces, human silhouettes pressing outward from inside the walls.

Adrian stumbled backward; the movement immediately stopped. His breathing quickened. “No…” He leaned closer carefully. The wallpaper remained still now, but faint bulges remained visible beneath it, as though something trapped inside the walls had tried to push through moments earlier.

A whisper suddenly emerged nearby, soft and almost inaudible : “Adrian…” He spun around violently. Nobody there; only hallway. Another whisper came, closer this time : “Adrian…” The voice sounded familiar: his father.

Adrian’s chest tightened instantly. “Dad?” Silence answered him. Then a nearby hotel room door slowly creaked open by itself: Room 217\. Darkness waited beyond the doorway. Adrian approached cautiously. The room inside looked strangely familiar: a small living room, a dim lamp, an old television, and children’s drawings hanging on the walls.

Adrian froze. The drawings were his. His childhood handwriting covered the paper. His breathing became shallow. “This isn’t possible…” He slowly stepped inside. The air smelled like old cigarettes and dust. A memory surfaced suddenly—his father sitting beside him at the kitchen table helping him draw superheroes.

Adrian turned toward the television. Static flickered softly across the screen, then the image changed to a home video. Young Adrian laughing; Daniel filming him. The sight struck Adrian like a punch to the chest. The video quality warped strangely as though the tape itself were damaged. Then Daniel’s voice emerged from the television—not from the past, but from now : “Adrian…”

The screen glitched violently. Daniel suddenly appeared terrified inside the recording as he looked directly toward the camera. “No matter what happens,” he whispered desperately, “do not trust the woman at the desk.” Static swallowed the image instantly, and the television exploded into white noise.

Adrian staggered backward, then noticed something horrifying. The room behind him had changed; the doorway leading back into the corridor was gone, and only a solid wall remained. Adrian’s heartbeat spiked violently. “No no no—”

The lights inside the room flickered rapidly. The children’s drawings hanging on the walls slowly changed, the smiling stick figures twisting into distorted human shapes with eyes scratched out and mouths stretched open unnaturally wide. A loud knock erupted from inside the closet: *THUD.* Adrian froze. Another knock: *THUD.* The closet door trembled slightly. Something inside was moving, breathing slowly, as wet scratching sounds dragged against the wood from the other side.

Adrian backed away carefully. Then the voice came again—his father’s voice, weak, painfully weak : “Help me…”

Tears instantly filled Adrian’s eyes. “Dad?”

The closet door handle slowly began turning. Adrian stood paralyzed. The voice continued : “Please…” The handle stopped moving, and silence filled the room again. Then a second voice whispered from inside the closet—a woman’s voice, soft, smiling : “He opened the door for us too.”

The closet door suddenly burst outward. The lights went black instantly. Adrian screamed and stumbled backward into darkness—and crashed violently into the hotel corridor again. He hit the floor hard, breathing frantically.

The hallway around him now looked even worse. The wallpaper hung in shredded strips. Black liquid dripped slowly from the ceiling. The lights overhead pulsed red like a dying heartbeat. Then Adrian noticed them: eyes—hundreds of human eyes blinking open beneath the wallpaper all around him, watching silently, following his movements. The entire corridor breathed softly around him. Far ahead in the darkness, a woman stood motionless at the end of the hallway: The Receptionist, smiling patiently. Beside her stood a door marked ROOM 404.

The lights flickered once. She disappeared. Then somewhere deep inside the walls, something massive began moving toward him.

### Chapter 7 — The Fall

The corridor came alive. The walls pulsed violently around Adrian as though something enormous moved beneath the structure of the hotel itself. The eyes embedded inside the wallpaper blinked rapidly, watching him, hungry. The lights overhead flickered blood-red in uneven bursts. Far away, somewhere beyond the endless hallway, pipes screamed deep within the walls like an animal dying underground.

Adrian pushed himself up from the floor. His breathing came in sharp panicked bursts. The massive sound moving through the walls grew louder, closer. Wet scraping noises dragged behind the wallpaper beside him, and then a bulge formed beneath the wall surface—something crawling inside, moving fast. Adrian stumbled backward instinctively, but the bulge followed him. The wallpaper stretched outward grotesquely as human-like fingers pressed against it from the inside. Then another bulge appeared farther down the corridor, then another, until dozens appeared. The entire hallway suddenly writhed around him; the walls were full of things.

The lights burst off, and darkness swallowed everything. For one terrible second Adrian heard movement everywhere at once: crawling, scratching, breathing. Then the emergency lights activated overhead with a dull crimson glow, and Adrian saw them: figures standing throughout the corridor. Tall, thin, motionless, their bodies looked wrapped in darkness itself, barely visible beneath the pulsing red light. No faces; only silhouettes watching him silently from every direction.

Adrian’s heartbeat nearly stopped. One of the figures tilted its head unnaturally sideways, and then all of them began walking toward him simultaneously. Not running, not rushing; slow, deliberate, as though they already knew escape was impossible.

Adrian turned and ran. The corridor stretched endlessly before him. Doors flashed past—312, 104, 817, 223—numbers changing impossibly each time he looked away. Behind him, footsteps echoed in perfect synchronization. The hotel groaned violently around him as wallpaper peeled from the walls in long wet strips, the eyes beneath them rolling frantically inside their sockets.

Adrian rounded a corner—and froze. The corridor ahead no longer existed; only Room 404 stood waiting at the far end of an enormous black void. The room door looked different now: ancient, rotting, covered in deep claw marks, as if something had tried desperately to escape from the inside. The brass room number hung crookedly above the frame.

The red emergency lights flickered violently. The shadow figures behind Adrian continued approaching slowly from the darkness, closer and closer. Then the receptionist’s voice whispered softly beside his ear: “You’re remembering.”

Adrian spun around; nobody was there. But suddenly, the corridor changed. For one brief horrifying moment Adrian saw another version of the hotel layered over reality itself: burned walls, collapsed ceilings, human bodies fused into the hallway like melted wax. The shadow figures now appeared human beneath the flickering light—or what remained of human, their faces twisted unnaturally, eyes hollow, skin split open. Every one of them stared directly at him.

Then reality snapped back again. Adrian staggered backward toward Room 404\. The key inside his hand suddenly became scorching hot; he cried out and nearly dropped it. Every moment of pain he carried inside himself flooded outward into the fracture. The Host absorbed it greedily, and the void pulsed stronger. “You fed me long before you arrived.”

Adrian collapsed to one knee gasping. Daniel grabbed him desperately. “Don’t listen to it\!”

The Host continued : “This place exists because human suffering creates doors.” The floating fragments around them shifted violently, and Adrian suddenly saw countless people trapped throughout the fracture: missing travelers, researchers, patients, all suspended inside endless nightmare loops, still alive, still conscious. The Host spread itself through every environment surrounding them. “I gave meaning to fear.”

Daniel shouted angrily into the void : “You destroyed them\!”

The Host’s shifting shape became still, then : “They destroyed themselves.” The words echoed everywhere, and for a terrible moment Adrian realized the entity believed that completely. It did not see itself as evil—only hungry, only incomplete.

The giant doorway of Room 404 slowly creaked open wider, spilling darkness outward across the fractured dimension. And inside the room, Adrian saw them: every missing victim, frozen in place throughout different moments of terror—some screaming, some crying, some staring blankly into nothing—entire decades of stolen lives trapped inside the room like preserved memories. At the center of the room stood a pedestal, and resting upon it, the final anchor key—blackened, cracked, pulsing faintly like a dying heart.

Daniel’s expression broke completely. “If you destroy it…” His voice trembled. “…this place collapses.”

Adrian stared toward the key. “And everyone trapped here?”

Daniel lowered his eyes. “They may disappear with it.”

The silence that followed felt unbearable. The Host moved closer again—massive, endless, patient. “You can stay.”

The void shifted around Adrian instantly, and suddenly he saw another reality: himself and Daniel sitting together peacefully inside their old apartment, laughing, safe, alive—no hotel, no trauma, no suffering. It was a perfect memory reconstructed by the fracture.

The Host’s voice softened. “You never have to lose him again.”

Tears filled Adrian’s eyes instantly. For one terrible moment, he wanted it. After seventeen years of grief, he wanted to stay. Daniel saw the hesitation in his face and quietly whispered : “This place only gives copies.”

Adrian looked toward his father. Daniel smiled weakly through tears—the first genuine smile Adrian had seen from him since arriving. “You still have a life outside this nightmare.”

The false reality surrounding them began decaying immediately. The apartment walls melted. Daniel’s smiling figure distorted. The illusion collapsed.

The Host’s voice deepened with anger : “YOU BELONG HERE.”

The void trembled violently as the floating environments began collapsing faster, fragments crashing into one another throughout the darkness. Reality itself was dying. Adrian slowly stood, his breathing steadied, then he walked toward Room 404, toward the final key waiting inside. And behind him, The Host screamed for the first time—a sound so enormous the fractured dimension itself began breaking apart.

### Chapter 13 — The White Door

The scream of The Host shattered reality. The fractured dimension convulsed violently around Adrian as he crossed the threshold into Room 404\. The door slammed shut behind him into instant silence—not peace, but absence. The room stretched infinitely in every direction now, larger than any structure should possibly contain. The ceiling disappeared into darkness above, and the walls bent unnaturally outward, breathing slowly like the inside of some enormous sleeping organism.

Fragments of countless stolen lives floated suspended throughout the room: a woman crying beside an endless hallway, a child trapped beneath a hospital bed, a man pounding against a locked elevator door while decades passed across his face in seconds. All frozen, all preserved.

Adrian walked slowly through the impossible room. The blackened anchor key rested ahead atop the pedestal beneath a weak flickering light. Every step toward it felt heavier, as though the room itself resisted him. The knocking surrounding the dimension grew louder: THUD. THUD. THUD. Now Adrian understood—something wasn’t trying to get into Room 404, something was trying to get out.

The darkness behind the walls pulsed violently. Human silhouettes moved beneath the surfaces like trapped insects beneath skin. Whispers flooded the room, thousands of voices speaking together: “Don’t leave us.” “Help us.” “It hurts.” “He sees everything.”

Adrian forced himself forward. The pedestal waited only a few feet away now. Then the room changed again. Suddenly he stood inside his childhood apartment. Warm sunlight poured softly through the windows, and the smell of coffee drifted from the kitchen. For one impossible moment, everything felt normal. Daniel sat at the dining table reading the newspaper—alive, healthy, smiling.

The visual trick nearly broke him. No horror, no distortion, no darkness—just home. Daniel stood slowly and approached him. “You don’t have to keep suffering anymore.”

Adrian’s chest tightened painfully. This wasn’t real; he knew that, but the fracture understood exactly where to wound him. Daniel placed a hand gently on his shoulder. “We can stay here.” The apartment around them glowed warmly, and outside the windows children laughed somewhere in the distance. No storm, no fear—only peace.

Tears filled Adrian’s eyes. After years of grief and isolation, the illusion felt unbearably tempting. “You’re not him,” Adrian whispered weakly.

Daniel’s expression softened sadly. “Does it matter?”

The room darkened slightly—only for a second, but it was enough. Adrian noticed the clock on the wall; its hands spun backward unnaturally. The shadows inside the apartment stretched too long, and Daniel… Daniel wasn’t blinking.

The illusion began cracking around the edges. Paint peeled from the walls. The sunlight outside dimmed. The smile on Daniel’s face slowly widened too far.

Adrian stepped backward immediately. “No.”

The apartment trembled violently, and Daniel’s voice deepened unnaturally : “You would destroy all of this?”

The illusion finally collapsed as the walls melted away into darkness. The apartment twisted apart like burning paper, and standing before Adrian now was not Daniel, but The Host. Its shape towered impossibly large inside Room 404, shifting constantly between forms too unstable for the human mind to process: corridors, faces, hands, endless screaming mouths, and black oceans of moving shadow. Every victim absorbed into the fracture moved somewhere inside it.

The Host’s voice thundered through the room : “I SAVED THEM.”

The suspended victims around the chamber began twitching violently, their faces turning slowly toward Adrian. “You preserve suffering,” Adrian whispered.

The Host moved closer as reality warped around its presence. “I preserve existence.” The floor beneath Adrian cracked open. Beneath the room lay an endless abyss filled with trapped consciousnesses falling forever through darkness. The Host spread across the void like infection. “When humans fear death… they beg for eternity.”

The room shook violently, large fractures spreading across the walls now as the entire dimension collapsed faster. Adrian looked toward the pedestal again; the final anchor key pulsed weakly, waiting. Behind him the doorway into darkness reopened slowly, brilliant white light pouring faintly through the cracks. The exit. The White Door.

The Host saw Adrian looking toward it, and its voice instantly changed—desperate now : “You cannot survive outside this place.” Visions flooded Adrian’s mind violently: the world beyond decayed by time, empty cities, his life erased, everyone he knew gone. “You already lost everything.”

The room groaned loudly around them as fragments of the fracture collapsed into emptiness throughout the void. Adrian stared at the final key, then slowly reached toward it.

The Host screamed instantly. Every wall inside Room 404 burst open, and thousands of arms erupted from the darkness reaching toward Adrian desperately—victims, broken consciousnesses, people trapped for decades inside the fracture. The room descended into chaos. The Host lunged forward, reality shattering around its movement.

Adrian grabbed the final anchor key. The instant his fingers touched it, pain exploded through his body. Every memory trapped within the fracture flooded into him simultaneously—decades of fear, loneliness, madness, people begging for escape, people forgetting their own names, people becoming part of the environments themselves.

Adrian screamed. The key cracked violently in his hand, and a massive shockwave erupted through the dimension. The Host roared in agony. The floating environments surrounding Room 404 immediately began collapsing inward: hospital corridors folded into darkness, laboratories shattered apart, and entire realities imploded into black void. The fracture was dying.

Daniel suddenly appeared beside Adrian again—not an illusion, but real, or as real as he could still be. His body flickered violently now, barely holding shape. “It’s working\!”

The White Door behind them slowly opened wider, spilling a blinding light across the collapsing room. But The Host rose again—enormous, wounded, furious—its body expanding throughout the fracture like spreading darkness consuming reality itself. “YOU CANNOT LEAVE.”

The floor beneath Adrian collapsed completely, and the room began falling apart around them. Daniel grabbed Adrian hard. “You have to go\!”

“What about you?\!”

Daniel smiled sadly. For the first time since Adrian found him, fear disappeared from his face. “I was never leaving this place.” The words hit Adrian harder than anything else.

The Host surged toward them through collapsing darkness, its countless voices screaming together in rage. Daniel shoved Adrian toward the White Door. “RUN\!”

Adrian stumbled toward the light, but stopped to turn back. Daniel stood alone inside the collapsing Room 404 while darkness consumed the fracture around him. The Host descended upon him like a storm. Daniel looked toward Adrian one final time—proud, peaceful.

Then he raised the remaining anchor key in his hand and shattered it. The entire dimension exploded, light consuming everything as The Host screamed. Reality tore itself apart, and Adrian fell forward through the White Door into blinding white emptiness.

### Chapter 14 — Exit

Cold concrete slammed against Adrian’s body. Air rushed violently back into his lungs as he crashed onto the floor coughing, disoriented and half-blind from the overwhelming white light. For several seconds he couldn’t move, couldn’t think; only pain existed. His ears rang violently, the sound of The Host screaming still echoing somewhere deep inside his skull.

Then, silence—real silence. Adrian slowly lifted his head. Dust drifted through the air around him as gray ash floated gently downward from the ceiling like dead snow. The smell reached him first: smoke, burned wood, decay. He forced himself upright slowly and realized where he was: Room 404\. But not the impossible fractured version—the real room, or what remained of it.

The hotel room looked destroyed by fire decades earlier. The wallpaper hung in blackened strips from scorched walls. Furniture lay collapsed and rotting beneath layers of ash. The ceiling above had partially caved inward, exposing broken pipes and hanging electrical wires. Rainwater dripped steadily somewhere beyond the darkness.

Adrian’s breathing slowed. “It’s over…” The words barely escaped his mouth. The nightmare environments were gone—no breathing walls, no shifting corridors, no endless void. Only ruin, only silence.

The old television near the corner of the room emitted faint static, its cracked screen flickering weakly despite not being plugged into anything. Adrian stared at it chimneys uneasily, then looked down at his hands. The anchor key was gone; only deep burn marks remained across his palm—proof that none of it had been a dream.

A deep grief settled slowly inside his chest. Daniel—his father was gone, this time truly gone. Adrian closed his eyes tightly, and for a moment he simply stood there in the burned remains of Room 404 while ash drifted silently around him.

Then the television crackled louder. Adrian turned sharply toward it. Static distorted violently across the screen, and for one brief second, he saw Daniel standing within the noise—not frightened, not broken, but peaceful. Then the image vanished. The television died completely, and darkness reclaimed the room.

Adrian swallowed hard, then slowly moved toward the door. The same rusted hotel door that had swallowed him into the fracture now stood slightly open. Black claw marks still scarred the surface deeply, but whatever waited behind it before was gone—at least he hoped it was.

Adrian placed his trembling hand against the door and slowly pulled it open. The corridor outside stretched silent and abandoned beneath weak emergency lighting. Gone was the impossible breathing hallway from before; now it looked ancient. Collapsed sections of ceiling exposed rusted pipes overhead, mold spread across the walls, and the faded carpet beneath his feet crumbled with age. The hotel felt dead—finally dead.

Adrian stepped into the corridor cautiously, every footstep echoing softly through the empty building. No whispers, no movement, only silence following him through the ruins. Several hotel room doors stood open nearby. Inside them Adrian glimpsed old furniture covered in dust and water damage. One room had completely collapsed inward, another still contained suitcases left untouched beside the bed, as though their owners had vanished instantly—abandoned lives frozen in time.

Adrian continued walking slowly toward the lobby. The farther he moved through the hotel, the heavier the atmosphere became—not supernatural, but grief-stricken, like walking through the corpse of something ancient. The emergency lights overhead flickered weakly. Then Adrian noticed something strange: the hotel walls no longer looked impossible, the architecture finally made sense, and the corridors remained finite and normal. Whatever the fracture had done to reality, it was weakening, or dying.

Adrian reached the main staircase, each step groaning beneath his weight as he descended toward the lobby level below. Darkness swallowed most of the lower floor; only faint moonlight entered through shattered windows near the entrance.

Adrian stepped carefully into the lobby and stopped immediately. Someone stood near the reception desk—motionless, waiting.

The Receptionist.

Her pale figure remained partially hidden within darkness, exactly where she had stood before. For several seconds neither of them moved. Adrian’s pulse slowed into cold dread. No. No, she should be gone too. The fracture collapsed. The Host was dead. Wasn’t it? The receptionist slowly stepped forward into the dim light. Her crimson uniform appeared faded now—old, covered in dust—but her smile remained unchanged: calm, professional, unnatural.

“You came back,” she whispered softly.

Adrian stared at her. “What are you?”

The receptionist tilted her head slightly, and for the first time since meeting her, sadness briefly crossed her face, or something close to sadness. “I stayed too long.”

A cold shiver moved through Adrian’s spine. The lobby lights flickered weakly overhead. The receptionist looked smaller somehow, less powerful, as though the collapse of the fracture had damaged her too. Behind her, the large mirror hanging near the reception desk reflected only darkness—no reflection, still none.

Adrian took a slow step backward. “I destroyed it.”

The receptionist smiled faintly. “You destroyed a doorway.”

The words hit him like ice water, and silence followed. Then somewhere deep within the hotel, a faint metallic knock echoed softly : *THUD.* Adrian froze instantly.

The receptionist closed her eyes briefly, almost disappointed. “The fracture remembers,” she whispered.

Another knock echoed through the building: *THUD.* Closer this time. The emergency lights flickered violently.

### Chapter 15 — Lost Time

The cold night air burned Adrian’s lungs as he stumbled across the parking lot. Behind him, GRIDHouse Hotel stood silent beneath the fog—no screams, no shifting walls, no crimson lights—only an ancient building rotting quietly beside an empty highway. Adrian bent forward, struggling to breathe. Rain no longer fell; the storm had vanished completely. Even the wind felt unnatural—still, dead.

He slowly turned back toward the hotel. Its neon sign flickered weakly through the darkness: GRIDHOUSE HOTEL. Several letters no longer worked, and the glow barely illuminated the cracked pavement surrounding the building. From outside, the structure looked far worse than before: massive sections of the upper floors had collapsed inward, balconies hung broken and rusted, and entire rows of windows were shattered or boarded shut. Vegetation crawled across portions of the exterior walls like nature reclaiming a corpse. The place looked abandoned for decades, maybe longer.

Adrian’s chest tightened painfully. Daniel had been trapped inside this nightmare all that time, alone. The realization hollowed something inside him.

He forced himself to keep moving. His car waited near the far end of the parking lot where he had left it earlier that night. At least, it should have. Adrian slowed suddenly, his pulse quickening. “No…”

The vehicle sitting beneath the dead parking lights barely resembled his car anymore. Rust consumed nearly the entire body. The windshield had shattered long ago. Weeds grew through the tires. A thick layer of dirt and dead leaves covered the roof, as though the car had been sitting untouched for years. Adrian approached slowly in disbelief. His reflection stared back at him faintly through cracked glass—older, exhausted, half-dead. He touched the hood carefully, and cold metal crumbled slightly beneath his fingers. The license plate remained barely visible beneath layers of corrosion. It was his car, but impossible; he had arrived only hours ago, hadn’t he?

Adrian staggered backward slowly. “No…”

The silence surrounding the highway felt enormous now, oppressive. He turned toward the road and realized the world itself had changed. The old highway signs had collapsed. Trees swallowed sections of the asphalt entirely. Telephone poles leaned broken beside the road. Nature had reclaimed everything; the world looked abandoned, or forgotten.

A wave of panic rose inside Adrian’s chest. He pulled his phone from his pocket immediately—dead, the screen wouldn’t even turn on. He searched desperately through the car’s ruined interior. The seats had decayed badly, mold spread across the dashboard, and the smell inside nearly made him gag.

Then Adrian found something resting on the passenger seat beneath layers of dust: a newspaper, yellowed with age. His hands shook as he lifted it carefully. The date printed across the front page made his stomach collapse: October 28th, 2039\.

Adrian stared at the numbers, his mind refusing to process them. “No…” The paper slipped slightly in his trembling hands. Sixteen years—sixteen years gone.

His breathing became shallow. The article beneath the date showed a photograph of GRIDHouse Hotel, or rather, what remained of it : LOCAL LANDMARK TO BE DEMOLISHED AFTER DECADES OF ABANDONMENT. According to the article, the hotel had officially closed in 2004 following multiple disappearances and structural collapse. Authorities never discovered what happened to several missing guests, including investigative journalist Daniel Lawrence.

Adrian’s knees nearly gave out. The fracture had not merely trapped people; it had erased time around them. He looked back toward the hotel slowly, the broken windows staring back like empty eye sockets. Somewhere inside that building his father had suffered for decades while the outside world continued moving forward without him, and now Daniel was gone, truly gone.

Adrian lowered his head. For several moments the only sound came from his uneven breathing.

Then, a faint melody drifted softly through the fog: jazz music. Adrian froze instantly. The same song—the same impossible song from the radio. Slow piano notes echoed faintly from somewhere near the hotel.

“No…” He backed away from the building instinctively.

The fog surrounding GRIDHouse Hotel thickened slowly, and then Adrian saw movement inside one of the upper windows: a silhouette, standing motionless behind shattered glass, watching him. His pulse thundered. The figure slowly tilted its head sideways, exactly like the things inside the fracture.

Adrian stumbled backward again. Then another silhouette appeared in a nearby window, then another, until dozens appeared—human shapes standing silently throughout the hotel ruins, watching him leave.

The fog shifted, and suddenly the entire hotel looked normal again. Warm golden lights glowed behind the windows. The balconies appeared restored. The neon sign burned bright red against the darkness: GRIDHOUSE HOTEL. The illusion lasted only a second, then the hotel returned to ruins.

Adrian’s breathing became frantic. The fracture wasn’t dead—it was wounded, sleeping perhaps, but alive.

A soft knock echoed nearby: *THUD.* Adrian turned sharply toward the sound; nothing was there, only fog drifting slowly across the abandoned highway. Then another knock came from inside the ruined car behind him: *THUD.*

Adrian stepped backward slowly. The fog thickened further around the parking lot, and visibility shrank rapidly. Shapes moved faintly within the mist—tall, human, waiting. The jazz music grew louder, and a woman’s voice began humming softly somewhere nearby: The Receptionist.

Adrian’s survival instincts finally took over. He turned and ran down the abandoned highway away from the hotel. His footsteps echoed through the fog while the music followed faintly behind him. The farther he ran, the quieter the world became, until eventually, only the knocking remained—slow, patient, following him through the darkness: THUD. THUD. THUD.

### Chapter 16 — The Next Guest

Fog swallowed the highway whole. Adrian ran blindly through the darkness, his lungs burning violently as cracked asphalt stretched endlessly beneath his feet. The world around him no longer felt real. The abandoned road twisted unnaturally through the forest while shadows moved silently between the trees beside him. Behind him, somewhere hidden deep within the fog, the jazz music continued—soft, patient, following him.

THUD. THUD. THUD. The knocking echoed steadily through the night—not louder, not closer, but constant, as though distance no longer mattered.

Adrian kept running until his legs finally collapsed beneath him. He crashed hard onto wet pavement gasping desperately for air. The fog drifted slowly around him in pale waves. Silence returned again—no music, no footsteps, nothing. For several long moments Adrian remained motionless on the cold highway staring upward into the black sky above. Clouds moved strangely overhead, too fast, like time itself accelerating beyond him.

Then headlights appeared through the fog ahead, and Adrian sat upright immediately. A vehicle approached slowly down the abandoned road, its engine humming softly through the darkness. Human, real—relief hit him so suddenly it nearly hurt. Adrian stumbled to his feet and waved desperately toward the approaching vehicle.

“Help\!”

The headlights slowed. An old sedan emerged from the fog and stopped several feet away from him, its driver-side window lowering halfway. A young man sat behind the wheel—mid-twenties, nervous eyes, with a travel bag resting in the passenger seat beside him. The stranger looked confused. “Jesus Christ,” he muttered. “You okay?”

Adrian opened his mouth to answer—then froze. Jazz music played softly from inside the car radio: the same song.

The stranger frowned. “Hey?”

Adrian’s heartbeat slowed into cold horror. “No…”

The young man glanced toward the fog behind Adrian. “I’ve been trying to find a hotel for the past hour. GPS keeps losing signal out here.”

Adrian stepped backward slowly. The fog shifted behind the sedan, and through the drifting mist, a familiar neon glow flickered faintly in the darkness: GRIDHOUSE HOTEL. The building stood fully restored once again. Warm golden light poured from the windows. The neon sign buzzed brightly against the night exactly as it had when Adrian first arrived—no decay, no collapse, no ruins—alive again.

The young driver smiled weakly in relief. “Oh thank God.”

Adrian’s entire body went numb. “No,” he whispered.

The driver looked back at him strangely. “What?”

Adrian stumbled toward the car desperately. “You can’t go there.”

The young man frowned. “What are you talking about?”

“That hotel—” Adrian stopped. How could he explain it? That reality itself waited inside those walls? That time dissolved there? That something ancient and hungry lived beneath Room 404?

The driver gave a nervous laugh. “Look, man, you clearly need help. I can give you a ride somewhere if you want.”

Adrian looked past him toward the hotel. The front entrance doors slowly opened, warm light spilled outward into the fog, and standing inside the lobby, The Receptionist waited—perfectly still, perfectly calm, smiling directly at Adrian. No age, no decay; exactly the same.

The young driver followed Adrian’s stare toward the building. “Looks open to me.”

Adrian’s chest tightened violently. The Receptionist slowly lifted one pale hand—inviting, welcoming. Behind her, Adrian glimpsed movement inside the lobby: guests sitting quietly beneath chandelier light, drinking coffee, reading newspapers—repeating, always repeating.

The driver shifted the car into gear. “Well… thanks for the warning, I guess.”

Adrian stepped forward desperately. “Listen to me.” The driver paused, and Adrian’s voice shook. “If someone offers you Room 404…” The words caught painfully in his throat. “…don’t open the door.”

The young man stared at him uncertainly for several seconds, then smiled awkwardly. “Right.” He rolled the window back up slowly.

The sedan drove forward through the fog toward the glowing hotel. Adrian stood frozen in the middle of the highway watching it disappear into the mist. The jazz music drifted softly through the night air, then silence. For several moments nothing happened—only fog, only darkness.

Then, the neon sign buzzed brighter. Adrian slowly turned toward the hotel one final time. Through the lobby windows he could see the young traveler approaching the reception desk nervously. The Receptionist smiled warmly. The chandelier lights glowed softly overhead, and the grandfather clock ticked unevenly: Tick. Tick. Tick Tick. Pause.

Then the receptionist slowly slid a rusted brass key across the desk: ROOM 404. The traveler hesitated briefly before reaching for it. The moment his fingers touched the key, every light inside the hotel flickered violently. The lobby windows darkened instantly. The silhouettes inside stopped moving.

And from somewhere deep within the building, three slow knocks echoed through the night: THUD. THUD. THUD.

The hotel sign flickered once, then the entire building vanished back into fog, leaving only darkness behind. Adrian stood alone on the empty highway while cold wind moved softly through the trees around him. Far away in the distance, the jazz music started again, and somewhere beyond reality itself, something waited patiently for the next door to open.
